Exploring The Value of Life: A Deep Dive into Sugaru Miaki's 'Three Days of Happiness'

by: @Reblando, @Marvin B. Bernardo , @Jamaica Umapas Sanico, @Lawrence James Abarro

A critically acclaimed author Sugaru Miaki, has received widespread recognition from his thought-provoking and emotionally charged novel titled "Three Days of Happiness". With over 4.36 out of 5 rating from goodreads, his novel received a manga adaptation 3 years since it was first published, marketed with a different title "I sold my life for ten thousand yen per year" under the serialization of Shueisha's Shonen Jump, known for publishing popular works such as one piece, naruto and spy x family. As it grows popularity, it's manga adaptation tooked notice by numerous YouTube celebrities one of which is Garn Maneetapho, better known as Gigguk who is a Thai-British YouTuber and podcaster who is known for his comedic rants and reviews on anime and otaku culture. During the span of its growing audience, it received a rating of 8.78 out of 10 under hundreds of thousand readers in MyAnimeList, becoming one of the top 50 manga in the platform surpassing other popular works such as hunter x hunter, hajime no ippo, one punch man and chainsaw man.

Set against the backdrop of a mysterious contract with the Visiting Agency, "Three Days of Happiness" introduces Kusunoki, a man who trades his remaining lifespan for a substantial sum of money. The novel navigates through the consequences of this decision, exploring Kusunoki's internal struggles, regrets, and moments of self-discovery. Miaki skillfully examines the human condition, dissecting societal expectations and personal desires with a keen eye. As the narrative unfolds, readers witness Kusunoki's encounters with various characters, each contributing to the layers of emotional depth in the story. Miaki's prose, translated into English, retains the poetic and contemplative essence of the original Japanese, creating an immersive experience for readers. The author utilizes vivid descriptions and introspective dialogues to provoke thought on existential themes, fate, and the pursuit of happiness. His concept of trading a span of life for money in his book is no different from what we experience in the real world, especially to the toxic society that people of Japan is currently carrying. The employees of Japan most or from most organizations, their entire life are spent inside their workplace, where there'll be no escape from their never ending workload unless they have hit their retirement age, where they can leave with all the money they're paid with from their entire 40 years of life and spend it bits by bits with all their remaining years knowing their end is no longer far from reach. Through poignant and often melancholic moments, Miaki paints a nuanced portrait of the human psyche, prompting readers to reflect on their own values and aspirations.

Three Days of Happiness is a book that you should not steer clear of as Sugaru Miaki delivers a powerful meditation on life's inherent struggles and the pursuit of happiness. The novel is a testament to Miaki's ability to craft narratives that resonate with readers on a profound level. As the story unfolds over the course of three transformative days, it invites readers to contemplate the choices they make in their own lives and prompts a reevaluation of the true essence of happiness. Sugaru Miaki's work is a poignant exploration of the human condition, leaving a lasting impression that lingers in the reader's mind long after the final page is turned.
